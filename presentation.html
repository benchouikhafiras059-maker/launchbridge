02 — The Problem
Fragmentation.
Not missing information. Not laziness. Five parallel systems, none of them talking to each other.
📋
Visa / OPT
Deadlines that cascade — missing one invalidates everything after it. No tool surfaces them alongside everything else.
💼
Job Search
Applications tracked in memory or spreadsheets. OPT timeline affects which jobs to prioritize — but job boards don't know that.
🏠
Housing
Can't commit to housing without a job. Can't sign a lease without an offer letter. A circular dependency with no resolution point.
📄
Documents
I-20, passport, OPT application, EAD card — status tracked in mental memory. Low-grade background anxiety.
💰
Finances
Budget planning deferred because no salary known. But planning is needed before the offer arrives.
🧠
Mental Overhead
Managing the fragmentation itself is a form of pressure that compounds everything else.

"The hardest part is that everything is connected, but it's not in one place."

04 — Design Argument
The user does not need
more information.
They need clarity.
LaunchBridge does not give advice. It surfaces what already exists — the user's tasks, documents, applications — in a structure that makes priorities obvious and progress visible.
📍
One place
Everything in one tab, not twenty.
🔗
Connected

⬆️
Prioritized
The app tells you what to do next. You don't have to ask.

08 — AI Direction Log
How I directed AI.
7 logged sessions. AI was a capable builder — not a capable designer. The quality of the output was bounded by the clarity of the brief.
The pattern that worked
1. Define the design intent first (from Nico's needs)
2. Give AI a specific, constrained brief
3. Evaluate the output against the design argument
4. Push back when it drifts to generic patterns
App scaffold — Vite + React + localStorage hook. Kept entirely.
SVG Readiness Ring — kept. Reweighted score to 50/30/20 myself.
OPT Countdown — kept state machine. Changed urgency threshold 7d→14d.
Focus Assistant — AI produced chatbot. Rejected. Rebuilt as rule-based panel.
Sample data — kept structure. Rewrote contacts for realism.
Onboarding — kept flow. Simplified OPT status labels to plain English.
Mobile responsive — kept all. Changed overlay pattern for sidebar on mobile.
09 — Records of Resistance
3 moments I rejected AI output.
❌ AI produced
A chatbot "Assistant"
Text input field, message history, typing indicator, suggested prompts.
✓ What I did instead
Rule-based priority panel
Nico doesn't need to ask the app what his priorities are. The app has the data. Show it.
❌ AI produced
Corporate blue palette
Deep navy, high-contrast blue buttons. Looked like a government portal.
✓ What I did instead
Indigo + slate system
Nico is already stressed. The tool should feel calming and personal, not institutional.
❌ AI produced
Equal-weight score (33/33/33)
Tasks, documents, and jobs weighted identically.
✓ What I did instead
Weighted 50 / 30 / 20
Job rejections he can't control shouldn't tank a score that reflects real progress.
10 — Post-Mortem
What worked.
✓
Starting with Nico, not the idea
The interview replaced my original plan. The pivot from "information tool" to "prioritization tool" came entirely from listening.
✓
Writing the Design Argument first
It gave me a fixed point to evaluate every AI output against. When the chatbot panel appeared, I knew it was wrong immediately.
✓
The OPT countdown banner
Strongest testing reaction. Expressing a deadline as a concrete number of days made it real in a way a checklist item never could.
✓
Resisting the chatbot default
The rule-based panel is more useful precisely because it requires no user prompt. Nico's data is already there. Show it.
What failed.
✗
Cross-section connections
Nico expected job tracker + visa tracker to feel linked. They don't. The thesis says everything is connected — the UI doesn't show it.
✗
Built before testing assumptions
Budget Planner and Housing Finder were built before knowing if Nico would use them. Testing showed they're secondary. Earlier feedback would have redirected that effort.
✗
Generic secondary pages
Pages where I gave AI more latitude feel like a general productivity app. Brief quality translates directly to output quality.
11 — Five Questions Reflection
Q1 — Who?
Nico. The difference between designing for him and designing for "an international student" is the difference between solving a problem and solving a category of problem. When the person has a name, every decision has a test.
Q2 — What problem?
Cognitive fragmentation under deadline pressure. Five parallel systems, no unified view, persistent background anxiety that something important might be slipping through.
Q3 — How did research change the build?
Significantly. I planned an information tool. The interview revealed Nico doesn't need information — he needs a mirror. The research didn't confirm the plan. It replaced it.
Q4 — Where did AI help / get in the way?
Helped: scaffolding, SVG ring, OPT state machine, sample data. Got in the way: chatbot interface, corporate palette, equal-weight score. AI knows what productivity apps look like. It doesn't know what Nico needs.
Q5 — What would you do differently?
Involve Nico during the build, not just before and after. Show early wireframes, catch the Budget Planner investment before committing to it. Be more deliberate about what I design versus what I let AI scaffold. The best pages were ones where I had a design intent before I gave AI the brief.
The Lesson
The tool I built is better than the tool I planned to build, because Nico exists and I talked to him.
Designing for a real person means accepting that your assumptions will be wrong. The research doesn't confirm the plan — it corrects it. That correction is not a problem in the process. It is the process.
