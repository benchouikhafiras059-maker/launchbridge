# Design Argument

> *Written before AI engagement. This is the standard against which everything else is evaluated.*

---

## Who is this person?

Nico Fertonani is an international student from Argentina finishing his degree in the United States. He is weeks away from graduation and living inside a period of his life where almost every major decision — where to work, where to live, how to stay in the country legally — needs to happen at the same time, with no margin for error.

Nico is not disorganized. He is not careless. He is someone being asked to manage an overwhelming number of interconnected systems — immigration law, job applications, housing markets, financial planning — simultaneously, with real consequences if he misses a step. He does this while finishing coursework, attending interviews, and trying to figure out what his life looks like in three months.

He has a laptop, a phone, a browser with too many tabs open, and a calendar full of things he hasn't started yet.

---

## What is broken for him?

The tools that exist were not built for Nico. Job boards don't care about his OPT deadline. Visa checklist PDFs don't know he has an interview tomorrow. Housing apps don't know he can't sign a lease until he has an offer letter. Everything he needs exists somewhere, but nothing talks to anything else.

The core problem is not missing information — it is **fragmentation**. Nico knows what he has to do. What he cannot do is hold all of it in one place, see it at once, and understand what to do *first*. The mental overhead of managing that fragmentation is itself a form of pressure that compounds everything else.

He said it directly:

> "The hardest part is that everything is connected, but it's not in one place. I have to think about jobs, visa deadlines, documents, money, and where I'm going to live, but I don't always know what to prioritize first."

That sentence is the design problem. One place. Connected. Prioritized.

---

## What does "helped" look like?

Nico opens the app and immediately knows two things: where he stands overall, and what to do next. He does not have to search. He does not have to remember. The system surfaces what is urgent, what is overdue, and what is at risk — and it does it through the structure of the page, not through notifications, alerts, or AI-generated advice.

"Helped" looks like reduced cognitive load on the worst days. It looks like Nico not waking up at 2am wondering if he missed something. It looks like one tab, not twenty.

It does not look like a chatbot. It does not look like a productivity app trying to gamify his anxiety. It looks like a dashboard that respects his intelligence, gives him real information, and gets out of the way.

---

## Why are you the one building this?

Because I am close enough to this problem to understand what it costs, and far enough from it to see what is missing. I have watched international students navigate this transition — the visa panic, the job search pressure, the housing scramble — and I have seen them reach for tools that were not designed for them.

I built this because Nico exists, and the tools he needed did not. I built it because the design problem was clear, specific, and solvable. And I built it because designing for a real person, with a real name and a real deadline, is the only kind of design worth doing.
