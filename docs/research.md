# Research Documentation

---

## The Person

**Nico Fertonani** — international student from Argentina, preparing for post-graduation transition in the United States. Completing his degree at SCAD, on an F-1 student visa, navigating OPT application requirements alongside job searching, housing planning, and financial preparation.

---

## Interview Notes

### Context

The interview was conducted in-person, conversationally. Nico was not given a list of questions in advance. The goal was to listen, not to validate assumptions. Notes were taken during and immediately after the session.

---

### What he said

**On the overall feeling of the transition:**

> "The hardest part is that everything is connected, but it's not in one place. I have to think about jobs, visa deadlines, documents, money, and where I'm going to live, but I don't always know what to prioritize first."

This was the clearest, most unprompted articulation of the design problem. He did not need to be led to it — it was the first thing he reached for when asked what felt hardest.

**On visa / OPT specifically:**

He described the OPT timeline as stressful not because he didn't know the deadlines, but because he couldn't see those deadlines *in relation to everything else*. Knowing he had to submit his OPT application 90 days before graduation was not the hard part — knowing how that deadline sat against his interview schedule, his housing search, and his coursework was what made it feel unmanageable.

**On the job search:**

He is actively applying. He described spending time on LinkedIn and job boards, but noted that managing applications — knowing which ones he had followed up on, which ones were still active, which ones had gone silent — required mental effort he felt he was spending in the wrong places.

**On housing:**

Housing was something he described as "the thing I keep pushing off" because he felt he couldn't make housing decisions until he had more clarity on where he'd be working. He understood those decisions were connected but had no system for tracking that connection.

**On documents:**

He was aware of which documents he had and which he needed, but keeping that awareness current was something he described as "always in the back of my head" — a low-grade background anxiety rather than a managed checklist.

---

## Observed Pain Points

| Pain Point | Category | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| No single view of all open tasks by deadline | Prioritization | High |
| OPT deadline not visible alongside job search | Visa / Job crossover | High |
| Document status tracked in mental memory, not a system | Documents | Medium |
| No way to see which decisions depend on which other decisions | Systems thinking | High |
| Housing decisions delayed by lack of job clarity | Housing / Job crossover | Medium |
| Application follow-up managed informally | Job tracking | Medium |

---

## Environment

Nico works primarily from his laptop. He is not looking for a mobile-first solution — he is sitting at a desk with multiple tabs open trying to manage a complex life transition. The environment is high-cognitive-load: multiple browser tabs, email, school portals, immigration service websites, job boards, all running simultaneously.

The design implication is clear: the tool needs to **consolidate**, not add another tab to the pile. It needs to be the thing that makes the other tabs unnecessary.

---

## What this research changed

Going in, the assumption was that Nico needed better information about the OPT process. What emerged was different: he already had the information. What he lacked was a way to hold all of it at once and understand what to do next. The pivot from "information tool" to "prioritization and consolidation tool" came directly from this conversation.

The Focus Assistant panel — which surfaces the top 3 priorities based on deadline and urgency rather than offering advice — was a direct product of this research. He didn't need guidance. He needed a mirror.
